# Iweriú — the Isle at the Edge of the World

The persistent setting for D&D 5e one-shots. Adventures planted here declare `World: worlds/dnd5e.md` in their hook. **GM: read `## The quiet joke` before first play — it governs how this world is spoken of at the table.**

## How it feels

An island so green it looks lit from inside, wrapped in a sea that ends the world. Mist is weather, omen, and door. The forests are older than the tribes and do not care about them; the bogs remember everything they are given. People are warm, loud, and generous around small fires precisely because the dark outside is so large. Wonder here is real and dangerous in equal measure — nothing beautiful is safe, and nothing safe is beautiful. Magic is not learned so much as *negotiated with*.

The age is early: bronze is a rumor from the eastern sea, stone and bone do the work, cattle are wealth, and the greatest engineering feat in the history of the world is currently a hill with scaffolding on it.

## The Isle

- **The Isle itself** — called simply *the Isle*, or in the oldest songs *Iweriú*, a name so old nobody remembers what it means. Green hills, black bogs, grey stone, white strands. No serpent has ever lived on it; the seers consider this significant and will not say why.
- **The Whitecow** — the broad slow river of the east, named for the horned goddess said to walk its banks at dusk. The richest valley of the Isle, and the holiest.
- **The Drowned Country** — east, under the sea. The elders' elders said you could once walk dry-shod toward the sunrise for days. The sea took it in one lifetime. Things still wash ashore that were made by no tribe of the Isle: tools of strange design, carved posts, once an entire oak house, roofbeam and all, gently, over a week. Nobody builds with its wood.
- **The Giant's Road** — in the far north, a causeway of thousands of perfect six-sided stone pillars marching into the sea, unfinished. The giant who built it meant to reach an enemy across the water. The seers say the enemy is still waiting.
- **The Hill Where Kings Are Named** — a low green hill in the center of the Isle that every tribe can somehow see from its own lands, which is impossible, and is not discussed.

## The Five Parts

The Isle is divided in five, and each part has a *gift* — a thing it is for. This is not politics; it is older than politics, and everyone simply knows it the way they know which way is north.

| Part | Gift | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **North** | Battle | Feuding cattle-lords, the finest fighters, the shortest tempers, the best wakes |
| **West** | Wisdom | The seer-lines and the deep druid groves; the sun drowns there nightly and they watch it go |
| **East** | Plenty | The Whitecow valley: cattle, barley, salmon, and the labor and wealth to raise the Mound |
| **South** | Song | Bards whose praise can make a chieftain and whose satire can raise blisters on his face |
| **Center** | Kingship | The smallest part and the axis of all of them; the Hill; neutral ground, on pain of the land itself objecting |

## The Mound on the Whitecow

The great work of the age, three generations of labor and not yet done: a passage tomb the size of a hill, ringed with kerbstones carved in spirals that seers read like weather. White quartz for its face is hauled from the southern mountains; the great stones come up the river on rafts of lashed hide-boats.

What is known (to everyone): the dead of the seer-lines will sleep in it, and its door is aimed at *one* sunrise — the morning of midwinter, when the year turns and light enters the dark of the earth like a returning king.

What is suspected (by druids, in private): the Mound is not a tomb that faces the solstice. It is an *instrument* that requires the dead, and what it does on that one morning has not been done before in the history of the world, and the seer-lines started building it in a hurry three generations ago for a reason they have not shared.

The building site is the crossroads of the Isle — every tribe sends laborers in rotation, every one-shot can plausibly pass through it, and anything lost, stolen, hidden, or prophesied can plausibly involve it.

## The Neighbors

They live in the hollow hills and are never called by any truer name, because names are invitations. They are not small, not winged, and not kind — tall as spears, beautiful the way winter is beautiful, older than the bogs. They consider the Isle theirs and the tribes to be interesting livestock.

- Every hollow hill, lone hawthorn, and unexplained ring of mushrooms or stones is a **door**. Do not build on one, sleep on one, or relieve yourself on one.
- They keep bargains to the letter and never to the spirit. They cannot lie outright and have never once needed to.
- They take: milk left souring for no reason, a harvest of one field blighted in a night, and sometimes a person — a harper, a beautiful child, a bride on her wedding night. Sometimes the taken come back, years later, unaged and wrong.
- Iron — the rare black metal that falls from the sky in stones — burns them. A family that owns a sky-iron knife passes it down like a kingdom.
- The druids' whole craft, beneath everything, is *diplomacy with the Neighbors*. The Mound worries them.

## Magic and the sacred

High fantasy, worn old and close to the ground — no wizard towers; power lives in *places, oaths, and blood*.

- **Druids** are the Isle's law, memory, medicine, and magic: they speak for groves, read the Mound's spirals, geld kings who fail the land, and cast every spell as if quietly reminding the world of an agreement it signed long ago. Their magic is real, full-strength D&D druidcraft wearing antlers and ash-smoke.
- **Geasa.** A geas is a magical taboo laid on a person — *never eat heron; never refuse hospitality; never strike a woman's son*. Breaking one turns your luck to water; heroes carry them like invisible armor with one missing scale. (Mechanically: the GM may offer a PC a geas as story treasure — advantage-flavored fortune while kept, real consequences when broken.)
- **Bards** work magic through praise, satire, and memory — a satire can blister a face; three verses of a lament can make it rain. The South licenses them the way other lands license armies.
- **The bog remembers.** What the bog is given, it keeps — swords, butter, kings. Things put in the bog with words said over them *stay put*. Things taken out of the bog bring their words with them.
- **The salmon knows.** In a dark pool under nine hazels swims a salmon that has eaten the nuts of knowledge. Every seer-line claims to know the pool. None of them do. Finding it is a standing hook.
- **Clerics and gods:** the Isle's powers are the river-goddess, the antlered lord of beasts, the triple lady of battle who appears as crows, the smith of the underhill — worshiped in groves and at fords, no temples. Clerics are god-touched druids and speakers-at-fords; domains map freely (Nature, Life, War, Death, Forge, Twilight).

## People and daily life

- Wealth is **cattle**; war is mostly *stealing them back*, loudly, with poetry about it afterward.
- **Hospitality is absolute law.** A guest at your fire is sacred for three nights; harming one is the crime the bogs were invented for. Adventurers survive on this.
- The dead are waked with drink, music, and competitive grief; the great dead go under mounds.
- Writing does not exist. **Memory is the technology**: bards hold genealogies nine generations deep, and a trained memory is worth more than a boat.
- Personal names, for flavor: *Áed, Bré, Cana, Dara, Étar, Fial, Lugán, Mórr, Nes, Órla, Rónal, Senán, Tuala*. Tribes are named for an ancestor or an animal: *the Heron People, the Sons of the Red Bull, the Nine Alders*.
- Adventurers are **wanderers between the parts** — bards on circuit, druids' errand-hands, exiles, cattle-guards, oath-bound champions — welcome everywhere for three nights exactly.

## Threats and mysteries

- **Wolves and winter** — the honest dangers, always available.
- **The Neighbors** — see above; most one-shot problems are, three layers down, a bargain someone should not have struck.
- **The Drowned Country stirs** — more flotsam every year, and lately: footprints on the strand at dawn, coming *out* of the sea, filling with water.
- **The Mound nears completion** — and midwinter comes every year. The seers have begun buying up sky-iron. Quietly.
- **The Giant's Road** — the far side is not Scotland-that-isn't-named-yet. The far side is where the giant's enemy lives. Occasionally, hexagonal stones on the near shore are found *warm*.

## The quiet joke

**GM eyes only — this section governs the telling.**

This world is Ireland, around 3200 BCE, the building of Newgrange — and that fact is a *treasure the players dig up*, never a fact the GM announces. The rules:

1. **Never say Ireland.** Never say Newgrange, Celtic, Gaelic, druid-of-history, or any real-world word. The world only knows itself as the Isle.
2. **Let the clues accumulate** — they are already planted above, in ascending order of aha: a green isle at the world's western edge · **no snakes, pointedly** · the five parts with their gifts · a giant's causeway of hexagonal stones in the north · bog-offerings and bog-bodies · bards, satire, hospitality law, cattle-raids, wakes · geasa · the Neighbors under the hills · a passage tomb aimed at the midwinter sunrise on a river of the white cow · and for the deepest cut, the name *Iweriú* itself — the reconstructed oldest name of Ireland.
3. **When a player's eyes go wide** — when someone says *"wait… is this Ireland??"* — the answer is never yes and never no. The answer is an NPC's puzzled look ("what is an *ireland*?") and the GM's smile. The world never breaks character; the players get to be *right* without being *told*.
4. The Drowned Country is the sunken land toward Britain and Doggerland beyond — same treatment: real geography wearing myth, never named.

# Image style

The standard style block. Every session-image prompt **starts from this
paragraph verbatim**, then adds the scene. One register, every image, so the
whole campaign looks like one world remembered by one painter.

> Painterly digital illustration in a muted, atmospheric style — textured
> brushwork, not photorealism, not anime. A neolithic world: turf-and-timber
> roundhouses, standing stones, hide boats, tools of stone, bone, and rare
> bronze; clothing of wool, leather, and furs with woven patterns. Landscape
> of intense wet greens, black bogs, grey stone, and low mist; light is
> firelight, overcast silver, or low golden sun — never bright blue skies.
> Mood: warm human firelight against a vast, damp, indifferent wilderness;
> wonder with an undertow of unease. No medieval elements: no castles, no
> knights, no plate armor, no heraldry. No text, no borders, no watermarks.

Guards, for whoever composes the prompt:

- **The disguise holds in images too.** Never name the real-world place or
  era in a prompt; no shamrocks, harps-as-symbols, or anything that labels
  the Isle. The landscape may resemble what it resembles — that is the
  players' aha to have, not the image generator's to caption.
- **Witnessed-only** (per the GM prompt): the scene described must be one the
  players played. The style block never overrides that fence.
- Characters appear as they were at the table: their gear, their wounds,
  their weather. The world has no polished heroes.
