# The Longest Wake — secrets

**Read before play. Never narrated. Revealed only by play, per the GM prompt. Database note: anything from this file that must be recorded live goes to the private log (`rpg.log_event`), NEVER `rpg.narrate`.**

## The bargain (the truth of everything)

Forty years ago, in a famine spring, young Mórr walked to the hollow hill at dusk and struck terms with the Gentleman of the Hill. Cut onto a tally stave, letter-exact:

> **"Prosperity on Fiacc's Ford and all at Mórr's fire, for so long as Mórr sits at that fire. In payment: one night of song from the finest voice at his fire, payable when he leaves it."**

Mórr read it twice, like everything. Then he understood — decades later, on his deathbed — what he had signed: the prosperity clause ends when he *leaves the fire*. Dying doesn't move him; **burying him does.** And the payment clause triggers the same moment.

So the Neighbors, precise beyond malice, enforce specific performance: each time the village buries him, they return the collateral to his chair by dawn — because while Mórr sits at his fire, prosperity legally continues, no debt falls due, and no term is breached. The corpse in the chair is not a haunting. **It is contract maintenance.**

The Gentleman's actual position (never volunteered): the arrangement has become embarrassing. Returning a dead man nightly is clerk's work, his house is being laughed at behind its hands, and he wants a clean conclusion more than he wants the song — which is the party's real leverage, if they discover it.

## Why Mórr whispers

Mórr's last living act was regret. What is left of him is one instruction: *the stave* — find the terms, so his people can settle honestly what he settled recklessly. He gave the stave to the bog decades ago with the words *"kept until Mórr's people need the truth of it"* — which is why the bog yields it to a party acting for the village and fights one acting for itself (`scenes.md` §The bog).

## The ninth dawn

Neighbor accounting: nine nights of an unpaid, unacknowledged debt with the debtor absent from his fire in spirit constitutes abandonment. At the ninth dawn Bré closes the ledger and the house moves from *maintenance* to *collection* — Nes, as the finest voice at that fire, is the payment. No one is kidnapped: she is honor-bound and half-willing, which should be worse to watch. This is the adventure's clock; the party's actions before Bré reaches the hill decide whether the hill scene is a negotiation or a rescue.

## Nes's arithmetic

Nes has known since the second failed burial. The missing verses she trims from "Mórr's Luck" are not modesty — singing the full song *at the fire* names the price aloud at the fire, and she fears that constitutes acknowledgment of the debt (she is right; Bré's quill moves the moment it happens). If a PC gets the full verses sung, the debt is acknowledged early: nothing bad happens immediately, but Bré thanks the singer — and the party now negotiates from a acknowledged-debt position at the hill, which is *cleaner*, not worse. Nes's fear and the actual consequence differ; that gap is hers to be relieved about.

## Fial's ledger

The stores run out tomorrow. Fial has been serving her own house's winter share for three days. If the wake ends tomorrow, the Ford survives lean; every extra day costs a family's winter. She will never say this — Étar knows and watches her not-say it.

## Bré's courtesy

Bré's guest-right re-entries are not freeloading; a clerk observing compliance must himself be flawlessly compliant, and the three-night rule taken to the letter *is* his little joke about the whole arrangement. If the party works this out and names it to his face, he is delighted (expressed as one raised eyebrow) and answers three direct questions with the letter of the truth. This is the cheapest good lead in the adventure and its best reward for cleverness.

## The aha layer (world-level, optional)

This adventure plants the world's breadcrumbs without pointing: the wake and its competitive grief, hospitality as absolute law, the hollow hill and hawthorn, the bog that keeps what it's given, the Mound upstream needing barley and quartz. If a player's eyes go wide at any of it — per the world doc's §The quiet joke — the answer is never yes and never no.
